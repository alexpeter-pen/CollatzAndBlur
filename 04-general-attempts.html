4 · General Attempts

What has been tried and why it’s tricky.

1) Search for cycles & divergence

  • Cycles: brute‑force searches rule out small nontrivial cycles; parity‑vector arguments encode possible cycles but explode combinatorially.
  • Divergence heuristics: random‑model estimates suggest overall contraction on average, yet residue spikes (via \(v_2(3n{+}1)\)) keep local behavior jagged.

Moral: naive global statements miss residue‑scale structure.

2) Congruences, trees, and parity vectors

  • Backward trees: expanding the inverse map builds enormous trees but offers little uniform control.
  • Residues mod \(2^k\): partial structure appears, yet without a uniform inequality one residue can stall progress.
  • Parity encodings: semiconjugacy to shift systems clarifies patterns but doesn’t yield a monotone potential.

3) Probabilistic & p‑adic views

  • Stochastic surrogates: treat \(v_2(3n{+}1)\) as “almost geometric”; this captures trends but lacks a hard certificate.
  • 2‑adic/3‑adic dynamics: rich local behavior, yet the mix of + and × frustrates a single invariant that controls the integer orbit.

What keeps breaking

  • Lens mismatch: multiplication is native to Mellin (log‑scale), addition to Fourier. Demanding both be sharp at once forces an uncounted toll.
  • No uniform drift: without paying that toll, one “bad” residue can neutralize an otherwise negative average.

This points to the missed ingredient: explicitly budget the lens switch (+ vs ×) with a small constant \(\rho\).

Bridge to the next page

On the next page we isolate the two channels (addition vs multiplication) and add a tiny, explicit budget \(\rho\) for switching lenses. That budget enables a finite residue certificate with a positive margin \(\delta\), giving a uniform one‑step decrease of \(V(n)=\log n + \varphi(n\bmod 2^k)\).
