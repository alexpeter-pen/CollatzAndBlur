What is a Lyapunov function?

For a discrete map \(x\mapsto F(x)\), a Lyapunov function \(V\) is a scale that drops each step: \[ V(F(x)) - V(x) \le -\delta \quad (\delta>0). \] The drop needn’t be huge—any uniform \(\delta>0\) controls long‑term behavior.

We work with the accelerated odd Collatz map, so “one step” means applying \(T\) at an odd number.

Our choice of \(V\)

We use \[ V(n) = \log n \, + \, \varphi\bigl(n \bmod 2^k\bigr), \quad n\text{ odd}, \] where \(\varphi\) is a small correction defined on the odd residues modulo \(2^k\).

  • \(\log n\) captures multiplicative scale—perfect for the halving and the factor 3.
  • \(\varphi\) compensates residue bumps from \(v_2(3n{+}1)\).
  • \(k\) tunes resolution: larger \(k\) = finer residue control (still finite).

We normalize \(\varphi\) (e.g., average zero) to fix the additive gauge freedom.

The inequality we enforce

With \(T(n)=\dfrac{3n+1}{2^{v_2(3n+1)}}\) and \(r\equiv n\bmod 2^k\), \(r'\equiv T(n)\bmod 2^k\), the change splits as

\(V(T(n)) − V(n) = [\log 3 − v_2(3n+1)\cdot \log 2] + \log(1+1/(3n)) + [φ(r′) − φ(r)].\)

After a tiny last‑bits blur we bound the small additive term by \(\rho\). The finite residue certificate then requires, for every odd residue \(r\),

φ(r′) − φ(r) ≤ −δ − [log 3 − v₂(3r+1)·log 2] + ρ.

If such \(\varphi,\delta\) exist with \(\delta>0\) (and a small explicit \(\rho\)), we have a uniform drop on every odd step.

How this controls trajectories

  • Each odd step reduces \(V\) by at least \(\delta\). Summing along an orbit forces \(\log n\) to decrease overall.
  • Once below a small threshold, the rest can be checked finitely (computer‑verifiable).
  • Even steps are already harmless (they halve \(n\)); our analysis is anchored on accelerated odd steps.

What to remember

  • \(V=\log n + \varphi(n\bmod 2^k)\) is the scale.
  • \(\delta\) is the certified margin; \(\rho\) is the small budget for switching lenses (+/×).
  • The certificate is finite: it’s just a system of linear inequalities on odd residues.
