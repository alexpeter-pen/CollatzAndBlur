5 · The Missed Ingredient

Addition and multiplication live in different lenses—switching between them has a cost.

Home ← Prev Next →

Two native lenses

  • Multiplication → Mellin/log lens. Scaling is linear on the log axis; multiplicative structure is stable there.
  • Addition → Fourier/linear lens. Translations are natural; additive structure is stable here.

Collatz’s odd step mixes the two: \(n\mapsto 3n+1\). No single lens can keep both parts perfectly sharp at once.

A tiny switching budget \(\rho\)

We record the lens‑switch “toll” by a small constant \(\rho\). With the accelerated step \(T(n)=\dfrac{3n+1}{2^{v_2(3n+1)}}\) and a Lyapunov scale \(V(n)=\log n + \varphi(n\bmod 2^k)\), the one‑step change splits as

\(\Delta(n) = [\log 3 − v_2(3n+1)\cdot\log 2] + log(1+1/(3n)) + [φ(r′) − φ(r)].\)

We bound the small additive term by \(\rho\) after a gentle last‑bits blur and enforce the residue certificate

\(φ(r′) − φ(r) ≤ −δ − [\log 3 − v_2(3r+1)\cdot \log 2] + ρ,\) for every odd residue \(r \mod 2^k .\)

If we can make \(δ>0\) with \(\rho+δ < \log(4/3)\) (or another explicit threshold), then \(V\) decreases uniformly on odd steps.

Where \(\rho\) actually comes from

  • Finite resolution: working modulo \(2^k\) groups many \(n\) together; we upper‑bound the tiny term uniformly on each class.
  • Blur window: a short, positive averaging (e.g., boxcar) over last bits smooths residue spikes just enough for a clean inequality.
  • Lens change: estimating an additive perturbation inside a multiplicative frame (or vice versa) incurs a principled, explicit toll.

\(\rho\) does not change the map—it book‑keeps the unavoidable uncertainty of switching lenses.
