What is the Collatz map?

Start from any positive integer n. If n is even, send it to n/2; if odd, send it to 3n+1. A convenient “accelerated” odd step compresses all the trailing divisions by two:

\(\large T(n) = \frac{\LARGE 3n+1}{\LARGE 2^{v_2(3n+1)}} \,\, (\large n \text{ odd}),\)

where \(v_2(m)\) is the exponent of 2 in \(m\). Iterating \(T\) only at odd numbers removes the boring halving runs and makes the arithmetic structure visible.

Why “accelerate” and look at residues?

The basic energy change is \[ \Delta(n) \;=\; \log\!\frac{T(n)}{n} \,=\, \log 3 \, - \, v_2(3n{+}1)\,\log 2 \, + \, \log\!\Bigl(1 + \tfrac{1}{3n}\Bigr). \] The large, residue–controlled part is \(\log 3 - v_2(3n{+}1)\log 2\); the tiny additive correction is \(\log(1+1/(3n))\). Watching \(n\bmod 2^k\) stabilizes the big term and sets us up for a small, explicit “blur” later.

Notation: \(v_2\) is the 2‑adic valuation; all logs are natural unless stated.

A tiny example

Starting at 7 the accelerated odd steps are 7 → 11 → 17 → 13 → 5 → 1, with exponents v₂(22)=1, v₂(34)=1, v₂(52)=2, v₂(40)=3, v₂(16)=4 at each odd step. This illustrates how the residue term \(v_2(3n{+}1)\) drives the main drop.

Historical note (one paragraph)

The problem is often attributed to Lothar Collatz (mid‑20th century) and appears under names such as the Syracuse or 3x+1 problem. Its charm is the gap between a child‑simple rule and a still‑open global statement.
