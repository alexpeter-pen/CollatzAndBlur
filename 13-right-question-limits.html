13 · Asking the Right Question

Precision vs. principled uncertainty.

Home ← Prev Next →

What are we really trying to prove?

Not that every odd step is “nice”, but that a uniform, certified drop holds for a Lyapunov scale \(V(n)=\log n + \varphi(n\bmod 2^k)\):

\(V(T(n)) - V(n) ≤ -δ \,\, \text{for all odd } n, \quad δ>0.\)

This is the right question: a finite, checkable inequality that tolerates tiny uncertainty.

The trap of perfect precision

  • Exact integers only: insisting on zero uncertainty forces us to resolve residue spikes perfectly—one bad class can stall drift.
  • Ad‑hoc fixes: without a framework, any successful bound risks being accidental and non‑portable.

Experience suggests the statement is true with a principled budget for small effects—recorded as a constant \(\rho\).

Principled uncertainty

  • Local blur: a short positive averaging on last bits formalizes limited resolution.
  • Uniform budget: the tiny additive \(\varepsilon(n)=\log(1+1/(3n))\) is bounded by \(\rho\) on each residue class (after blur).
  • Finite witness: the target becomes a system of linear inequalities on \(\varphi\) indexed by odd residues.

We avoid naming any specific hidden structure; we only pay a small, explicit uncertainty cost.

Why this reframing matters

  • Asks for a finite certificate, not an omniscient formula.
  • Explains prior dead‑ends: the right question must tolerate controlled blur.
  • Connects analysis to computation: inequalities are auditable with interval arithmetic.
