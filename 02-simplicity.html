The rule in one breath

For a positive integer n: even ➜ n/2, odd ➜ 3n+1. Using the accelerated odd step, we define \(T(n)=\dfrac{{3n+1}}{{2^{{v_2(3n+1)}}}}\) (applied only to odd n).

This removes long halving runs and exposes the arithmetic signal we care about.

Simple ≠ easy: last bits steer the drop

The basic “energy change” for an odd step is \[\Delta(n)=\log\frac{{T(n)}}{{n}}=\log 3 - v_2(3n{{+}}1)\,\log 2 + \log\Bigl(1+\tfrac{{1}}{{3n}}\Bigr).\] The small term \(\log(1+1/(3n))\) is harmless; the main driver is the residue–dependent exponent \(v_2(3n{{+}}1)\).

Already modulo \(16\) the last four bits of \(n\) create uneven drops:

This jaggedness is the “noise” you feel when plotting Collatz trajectories: nearly identical inputs can have very different single‑step drops.

Why this matters

  • Local simplicity: the update rule is tiny.
  • Global difficulty: the residue term swings by several bits, masking average drift.
  • Foreshadowing: a tiny, explicit blur over nearby last bits will smooth the swing just enough to reveal a uniform decrease.

On page 6 we’ll peek at \(k=13\), where there are \(2^{{12}}\) odd residues; even there, a gentle blur stabilizes the inequality.
